# Biography

I grew up in the wake of one of the largest national systems and operations failure; after Hurricane Katrina made landfall fifteen miles from my home, levees not built to withstand Category 3 storm surge collapsed, communications between all levels of government faltered, and FEMA's response lacked the speed and scale to address the emergency. This disaster underscored the critical impact of effective decision-making, problem-solving, and model-building and has driven my passion for studying how to build practical models that tackle complex real-world problems using operations research.

I decided to spend my life pursuing operations research, sophomore year of my undergraduate degree. I switched from mechanical engineering to industrial engineering (which had OR courses) to pursue more big picture problems. From a young age I knew I was more of a big picture, strategic thinker, I liked to see the whole system of things and solve problems by making large changes that would ideally solve a problem at the fundamental level. When I was young, I watched rocket engine tests with my dad and mom, both engineers at Stennis Space Center, and was blown away by the sheer strength of the engine's propulsion and the magnitude of the system required to pull off a successful test. Throughout undergrad, I worked in Dr. Liang’s lab, focusing on Carbon Nanotube Laminates. This research has shown me the importance of optimizing systems, even in highly specialized processes, and seeing meaningful real-world success there cemented my commitment to pursue more research in graduate school. It was this resolve that led me to Dr. Newman’s program at Colorado School of Mines. She is a renowned expert in the world of OR, particularly when it comes to integer programs and, most importantly to me, practical solutions.
